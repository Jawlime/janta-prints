How to Prepare Print-Ready Artwork (And Why It Matters)

JP
Janta Prints Team
Kisumu, Kenya · Mar 2026
📁

Every week, our production team receives files that delay print jobs — not because the client's design is bad, but because the file isn't prepared for print. This guide fixes that. Bookmark it and share it with your designer.

Why print-ready matters

Digital screens display at 72–96 DPI. Print requires 300 DPI minimum. A file that looks sharp on your laptop can print as a blurry mess — and there's no way to fix it after we've run the job. Preparing your file correctly before sending saves time, money, and frustration on both sides.

1. Resolution: 300 DPI minimum

DPI (dots per inch) determines how sharp your print is. For most print work — flyers, business cards, brochures, jersey prints — you need 300 DPI at the final print size. For large-format items viewed from a distance (billboards, building wraps), 72–150 DPI at print size is usually acceptable.

Quick check: In Photoshop → Image → Image Size. If your resolution is below 150 DPI and "Resample" is unchecked, the file won't print well. Don't just increase the DPI with Resample on — that adds fake pixels and won't improve quality.

2. Colour mode: CMYK, not RGB

Screens use RGB (red, green, blue) light. Printers use CMYK (cyan, magenta, yellow, black) ink. RGB colours that look vivid on screen — especially bright blues and purples — can look muted or different when printed in CMYK. Always design in CMYK if you know the file is going to print.

If you've designed in RGB, convert to CMYK in Photoshop (Image → Mode → CMYK Color) before sending. Check how the colours look after conversion — some will shift and may need adjusting.

3. Bleed and safe zones

Bleed is extra artwork that extends beyond the edge of the final cut size — typically 3mm on all sides for standard print, 5mm for larger items. This ensures that if the cutting is even slightly off, you don't end up with a thin white border on the edge.

The safe zone (also called the keep-away area) is the opposite — keep all important content (text, logos) at least 3–5mm inside the trim edge, so nothing gets accidentally cut off.

4. File formats: what to send

  • PDF (print-ready) — The gold standard. Export from InDesign, Illustrator, or Photoshop as PDF/X-1a or PDF/X-4 with bleed marks.
  • AI / EPS — Native Illustrator files. Good for vector artwork. Make sure to outline all fonts.
  • PNG / TIFF — Acceptable for raster artwork. Must be 300 DPI. PNG preferred over JPEG for logos (no compression artefacts).
  • JPEG — Acceptable for photos only, at the highest quality setting. Never JPEG for logos or artwork with text.

Outline your fonts. If you're sending an AI or EPS file, convert all text to outlines (Type → Create Outlines) so we don't need the same fonts installed. Missing fonts = wrong font = reprint.

5. Use our Creative Studio before sending

Our free Creative Studio has a Crop & Resize tool where you can set exact dimensions and DPI, a Format Converter to export in print-ready formats, and a Background Remover for logos. Use it to do a final check before uploading your artwork to a quote.

Quick checklist

  • ✓ 300 DPI at final print size (150+ for large format)
  • ✓ CMYK colour mode
  • ✓ 3mm bleed on all sides
  • ✓ Fonts outlined or embedded
  • ✓ Important content 3–5mm inside trim edge
  • ✓ File format: PDF/X, AI (outlined), TIFF, or PNG
  • ✓ File named clearly: ClientName_ProductName_v2.pdf
