Full Sublimation vs Screen Printing: Which Is Right for Your Kit?

JP
Janta Prints Team
Kisumu, Kenya · Mar 2026
⚽

If you're ordering jerseys or branded sportswear for the first time, you'll quickly run into this question: sublimation or screen printing? The short answer is: for most football kits and sports jerseys, sublimation is the better choice. Here's why — and when screen printing makes more sense.

What is sublimation printing?

Sublimation (full-colour dye sublimation) infuses ink directly into the fabric fibres using heat. The result is a print that is part of the fabric itself — it won't crack, peel, or fade with washing. The print can cover the entire garment in any colour combination, with no colour limit.

Sublimation only works on white or light polyester fabric. For sports jerseys, this is perfect — sportswear is made from polyester because it's moisture-wicking and durable.

What is screen printing?

Screen printing presses ink onto the surface of the fabric through a mesh screen. Each colour in the design requires a separate screen and a separate pass. It works on almost any fabric colour and material — cotton, polyester, blends.

Screen printing is durable, but the print sits on top of the fabric rather than in it. Over time, especially with sportswear that's washed frequently, a screen-printed garment can crack and fade where sublimation won't.

Our recommendation for sports kits: Go with sublimation every time. No colour limits, full-garment design, better long-term durability, and you can print each player's name and number individually without a price increase per colour.

When sublimation wins

  • Full-colour, multi-colour, or complex designs (gradients, patterns, photos)
  • Sports jerseys, football kits, rugby kits, athletics gear
  • Designs that cover the whole garment (sleeves, collar, back panel)
  • Per-player customisation (each jersey has a different name/number)
  • Polyester fabric only — this is standard for performance sportswear

When screen printing wins

  • Dark or non-white garments (sublimation won't work on dark fabric)
  • Cotton t-shirts and casual wear
  • Simple 1–3 colour logos or text on a garment
  • Very large quantities (1000+) where the screen setup cost becomes negligible
  • Vintage or textured print effects

Heat transfer vinyl (HTV): a third option

HTV cuts vinyl and heat-presses it onto the garment. It works on dark fabrics and any material, and is the most common method for small runs of one or two jerseys with simple lettering. It's not as durable as sublimation for sportswear, but it's fast and cost-effective for name and number patches on existing kits.
